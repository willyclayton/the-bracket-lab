Most bracket models look at numbers. The Scout watches the film.

## The Approach

The Scout is a large language model (Claude) given a structured scouting brief for every matchup. For each game, it gets six data points that don't fit neatly into a regression:

- **Coaching tenure and tournament experience** -- first-year tournament coaches historically underperform their seed
- **Roster age** -- senior-heavy teams close out tight games; freshman-heavy teams crack
- **Injury reports** -- pulled from beat reporters and official designations
- **Last 10 games and conference tournament results** -- recent form matters more than November wins
- **Travel distance and rest days** -- a 12-seed flying cross-country on two days rest is a different team
- **Clutch performance** -- record in games decided by five points or fewer

The model gets a structured analyst role, not an open-ended prediction prompt. It returns a scouting report, key factors for each side, an X-factor, and a confidence-weighted pick -- all as structured JSON that feeds directly into the bracket.

## Why an LLM?

Efficiency ratings exist. Seed-based models exist. The Scout's bet is on **contextual synthesis** -- the ability to weigh that a 5-seed's All-American just returned from injury, their coach is 7-2 in close tournament games, and they're playing 40 miles from campus. No regression captures all of that at once.

## Honest Limitations

LLMs can hallucinate player stats (mitigated by injecting structured data, not asking the model to recall it). They can also show bias toward brand-name programs -- the prompt explicitly corrects for this. And once picks lock March 19, there's no updating. The Scout's read on the field is frozen in time.
