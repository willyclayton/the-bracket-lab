The Historian doesn't predict the future. It looks up the past.

Fifteen years of tournament data — every seed, every efficiency rating, every upset — contains a library of archetypes. High-seed, slow-tempo, defense-first programs from power conferences behave the same way in March regardless of roster names or graduation years. The transfer portal changes faces. It doesn't change the archetype.

## Finding the Twin

For every team in the 2026 bracket, The Historian runs a similarity search against its historical database. Each team is represented as a normalized vector across six dimensions:

- Seed
- Adjusted offensive efficiency (AdjOE)
- Adjusted defensive efficiency (AdjDE)
- Tempo (possessions per 40 min)
- Strength of schedule
- Coaching tenure

Euclidean distance across that vector finds the closest historical twin from 2010-2025. A 2026 team that looks like the 2019 version of a certain program gets that team's tournament outcome as its base prediction.

## The Archetype Library

Across 15 years, four archetypes repeat reliably:

**The Chalk Giant** — #1 or #2 seed, dominant efficiency margins, tends to reach Final Fours but doesn't always win it all. Championship rate: ~40%.

**The Reliable 4** — #3/#4 seeds with balanced efficiency and experienced coaches. Consistently outperform their seed in the first two rounds, disappear in the Elite 8. Pool-killers.

**The Mid-Major Disruptor** — Non-power conference team, elite defense, slow tempo. Dangerous in Round 1, especially against offensive teams that don't adapt well to pace changes. Historical 12-5 upset rate: 37%.

**The Glass Cannon** — Explosive offense, weak defense. High ceiling, fragile floor. The most common Sweet 16 exit type in the dataset.

## Making the Pick

For each matchup, The Historian compares both teams' historical twin outcomes and resolves conflicts using win rate by matchup type. A mid-major disruptor vs. a glass cannon has resolved in favor of the disruptor 6 out of 10 times historically. The Historian picks accordingly.

It doesn't call upsets for narrative reasons. It calls them when history says that specific matchup pattern favors the lower seed.

## Where It Gets Interesting

The Historian's champion pick comes from identifying which archetype combination has the strongest historical path through the bracket as seeded. Some archetypes win from the 1-line. Others have historically overachieved from the 3 or 4 line when the bracket broke right.

The 2026 draw matters as much as the teams.

## Limitations

The dataset only goes back to 2010. Rare matchup combinations have thin samples. The transfer portal era (post-2021) has meaningfully changed team construction in ways that older data can't fully account for — the model applies a recency weight but it's an imperfect adjustment.
