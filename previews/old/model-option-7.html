Long-form methodology as editorial prose

The Scout approaches March Madness the way a veteran coaching staff breaks down game film -- except it does it for every matchup in the bracket, in a fraction of the time, with no coffee breaks.

The Approach

The Scout uses large language models to analyze each matchup the way a coaching staff would break down film. It evaluates tempo, defensive efficiency, key player matchups, coaching tendencies, and situational factors like tournament experience. Each game gets a structured analysis prompt, and the model picks the team with the stronger overall matchup profile.

Every team tells a story on film. The Scout reads sixty-three of them before tip-off.

Unlike pure statistical models, The Scout incorporates qualitative factors that resist quantification: the body language of a team riding a late-season win streak, a coach's documented tendencies in elimination games, the way a zone defense tends to crumble against specific offensive sets in high-pressure moments.

The result is a bracket that looks reasonable at first glance -- no 16-seeds in the Final Four -- but carries distinctive convictions. The Scout picked 6upsets in the first two rounds, getting 4correct.
