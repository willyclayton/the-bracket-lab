## The Problem With Chalk

The national bracket average follows seeds more than 70% of the time in Round 1. Everyone takes the same two 12-over-5 upsets. Every Final Four has at least two 1-seeds. By the Sweet 16, most brackets are practically identical.

That's fine if you want to finish in the middle of your pool. If you want to *win* your pool, you need differentiation. One correctly called upset that nobody else in your pool picked is worth more than five correctly called 1-seed wins.

The Chaos Agent is optimized for pool-winning potential, not raw accuracy. It accepts more wrong picks in exchange for the right kind of right picks.

## Upset Vulnerability Scoring

For every early-round matchup, the higher seed gets scored on five factors:

- **Efficiency gap** -- how much does the lower seed's KenPom rating close the gap? A 12-seed in the top 40 of adjusted efficiency gets flagged immediately
- **Tempo mismatch** -- if the higher seed needs pace and the lower seed plays slow, that's structural upset territory
- **Experience differential** -- a 5-seed with three freshman starters vs. a 12 with a senior backcourt is a setup
- **Bracket position** -- not all 5-12 matchups are equal; some regional draws and game slots produce more upsets historically
- **Brand bias correction** -- the model explicitly downweights brand-name programs when the data is ambiguous. Duke has lost in Round 1. Kansas has lost to 8-seeds. History doesn't care about tradition.

Vulnerability score above 65: take the upset. Above 80: high confidence.

## The Math

The result is a bracket with roughly 40% more first- and second-round upsets than average. Most will be wrong. That's by design.

Expected value analysis of historical pool data shows high-upset strategies beat chalk in about 35% of head-to-head matchups -- but when they win, they win by a lot. High risk, high reward. Built for pools, not predictions.

## Honest Limitations

In years where chalk runs deep through the Sweet 16, this model gets crushed. It also doesn't fully cascade upsets downstream -- a manual consistency pass prevents structural impossibilities, but the bracket carries more variance than a path-dependent model would.
