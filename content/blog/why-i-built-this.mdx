This started with a phone call.

My buddy Jack Gorman floated an idea: what if you ran a bunch of AI models against a March Madness bracket and tracked how they did? Not as a gimmick -- as a real experiment. Different approaches, locked picks, verified results.

I couldn't stop thinking about it.

## The Question

I work in AI consulting. I spend my days helping companies figure out what these tools can and can't do. But I'd never stress-tested them against something this clean -- 63 games, public data, binary outcomes, a universal scoring system. March Madness is a near-perfect AI benchmark hiding in plain sight.

So I wanted to know: **do different AI approaches actually produce meaningfully different predictions?** And if so, which ones hold up when the games start?

## The Build

Standing up the site was the easy part. Claude Code and I got a working version live in under a week -- eight models, a scoring engine, bracket visualizations, a blog, a leaderboard, all running on Next.js and Vercel. The tool I used to build the thing *is* the thing I'm testing.

But "working" and "good" are different things.

The real time went into the grind after that. Iterations on iterations -- reformatting how brackets render, reworking the data structures until they were clean enough to trust, documenting every decision so I could retrace my steps. This wasn't "make me a cool March Madness site." This was build, look at it, realize something's off, adjust, plan the next piece, sometimes take a step back, then iterate again until it felt right.

Not perfect. I don't think it's perfect. But I got it to a place where I look at it and think: yeah, that's polished. That's something I'd actually share.

## What I'm Trying to Learn

A few things, honestly:

**How far can I push AI as a prediction tool?** Not toy demos -- real predictions, locked in advance, scored against reality. No take-backs.

**Where do models agree and disagree?** When eight different approaches all pick the same team, that's a signal. When they diverge, the *why* is usually more interesting than the *who*.

**Can this actually help someone win their pool?** I built this hoping my friends can use it to fill out better brackets. Not mine, though -- I'll fill mine out the old-fashioned way and probably lose to someone who picked based on mascot vibes.

## The Rules

Every model has an ESPN Tournament Challenge entry locked before tip-off. That's the timestamp proof. No retroactive edits. Results are sourced from ESPN -- never generated, never fabricated.

The models predict. The results are real. That's the whole integrity layer.

Thanks to Jack for the idea. Let's see what happens.
